**Leash** is the assistant harness: the product surface where you chat, approve actions,
use tools, load skills, connect MCP servers, and operate your personal AI. It runs on your
own hardware and keeps the user-facing experience coherent even when the work spans several
models, tools, and devices.

**Mycelium** is the connected runtime underneath. It ties together the local model serve,
typed memory, retrieval, skills, tool groups, delegated compute, and the encrypted mesh so
Leash behaves like one assistant instead of a pile of disconnected features.

Taken together, that means Leash can perceive your world locally and reason above the weight
class of any single device: it pulls in private context from the surfaces you already use,
then places heavier work on the right peer when one machine is not enough.

## Harness versus runtime

It helps to keep the two words straight:

- **Leash** is the flagship assistant harness — the main agent, dashboard, approvals flow,
  chat loop, and operator console in `apps/web`, plus the desktop and mobile clients that
  present the same assistant to the user.
- **Mycelium** is the runtime boundary — the serve and model-alias layer, Hypha for pairing
  and delegated compute, the skills/tool/MCP backing stores, and the `packages/*` that
  implement senses, mind, mesh, memory, and shared logic.

Treating Leash as the whole system would hide that boundary. The architecture, the mesh, and
the memory loop are Mycelium concerns; the chat, approvals, tasking, and settings surfaces are
Leash over them.

## Why Leash is general-purpose

Leash is not "general-purpose" because it claims to do everything. It is general-purpose
because the harness is extensible:

- tools expose concrete capabilities
- skills add reusable workflows and routing hints
- MCP servers add external systems and typed forms
- specialist agents let the main assistant delegate focused sub-tasks

That is why a private health-record workflow belongs here without redefining the product as a
health app. Health is the proof that a private, extensible assistant can handle sensitive,
high-context work; it is not a separate category bolted onto the side.

## What makes it distinct

Three constraints shape the product:

- inference is QVAC-only
- the system is expected to work offline after warm-cache
- the runtime is allowed to use multiple of your own devices, but never third-party cloud AI

That combination changes the architecture. Models live in a local serve. Mesh routing is
between your machines. Retrieval is against local files and stores. When the system needs
another GPU — or a modality this device can't serve — it borrows one from a paired peer, not
from a hosted API.

## Private mesh and public mesh

Mycelium's mesh is not one flat trust zone.

- On your **private mesh**, your own devices delegate compute to one another for free.
- On a **public mesh**, outside providers can expose compute as free or paid, with routing and
  settlement policy handled by the runtime rather than by a different product.

That matters because the assistant experience stays the same while the trust and economy tier
changes underneath it. Leash is still one assistant. Mycelium is what lets it scale from one
device, to your own device swarm, to a broader compute market.

## What the user is actually getting

From your point of view, Leash offers:

- grounded chat over private notes, activity, and past conversations
- local perception over voice memos, photos/screenshots, OCR text, recent screen activity, and the proactive feed
- delegated compute and modality borrowing over a paired device mesh
- extensibility through tools, skills, MCP servers, and specialist delegates
- a private mesh that is free between your own devices, plus optional free-or-paid public mesh routing
- typed memory plus retrieval-backed recall, and a nightly loop that adapts a personal model
- local speech, vision, and image paths
- a reproducible workspace where the core runtime is inspectable
