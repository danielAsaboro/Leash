---
title: "Leash and its surfaces"
description: "How the flagship interface is split into chat, Brain, Feed, Services, research, and settings rather than one flat app."
---

Leash is the flagship interface for Mycelium, but it is not one undifferentiated screen.
It is a cluster of operator and user-facing surfaces that expose different parts of the
platform.

## Why the surface split matters

If everything were forced into chat, the system would be harder to inspect, harder to
operate, and harder to trust. Mycelium exposes several surfaces because the runtime does
several different jobs.

## The main surfaces

- **Chat** is the conversational front door: grounded answers, tools, approvals,
  multi-step reasoning, and a plan mode that drafts a plan for you to approve before it runs.
- **Brain** is the configuration and introspection surface for memory, skills, tools,
  MCP, prompts, models, growth, and forage.
- **Models** is one filterable browser over the full catalog, with per-model compute,
  context-size, and mesh-sharing controls.
- **Tasks** is the unified work log across your to-dos, the newsroom pipeline, and scheduler
  runs.
- **Economy** is the ledger for borrowed and served compute: balances, the peer market, and
  on-chain receipts.
- **Feed** is the publishing surface for The Understory.
- **Services** is the runtime console for daemons.
- **Research** is the detached long-running web research surface.
- **Settings** is the machine-local operations area: a tabbed console for storage, devices
  and mesh membership, permissions, and an about panel.

## Why Brain exists separately from Settings

Brain configures the assistant's behavior and knowledge. Settings configures the local
machine, mesh memberships, credentials, and permissions. They look adjacent in the UI,
but they solve different operator problems. Settings is itself tabbed —
[Storage / Devices / Permissions / About](/guides/use-brain-and-settings) — because each
answers a different machine-local question.

## Why Economy and Models are first-class

Borrowed compute and the model catalog used to hide inside Services. They surfaced as their
own pages because each is a place you *decide* something: the [Economy](/guides/economy) ledger
is where you read what serving and borrowing cost, and [Models](/guides/use-the-model-catalog)
is where you choose what to run, how to run it, and what to share. Those are recurring operator
choices, not one-time setup, so they earn a surface.

## Why Feed is part of the showcase

Feed is where the project stops looking like "yet another local chat app." It shows that
the same local runtime can produce a researched, reviewed, and published artifact with
its own evidence trail, not just answers in a message bubble.

## Why Services is visible

Most apps hide their daemons. Mycelium makes them visible because the runtime is honest
about being a set of cooperating local processes. That honesty is useful operationally
and also helps explain what the system really is.
